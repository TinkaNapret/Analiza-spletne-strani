About the author

Inio Asano

123 books2,368 followers
Inio Asano (浅野いにお, Asano Inio) is a Japanese cartoonist. He is known for his character-driven stories and his detailed art-style, making him one of the most influential manga author of his generation.
Asano was born in 1980 and produced his first amateur comics as a teenager. His professional debut happened in 2000 in the pages of the magazine Big Comic Spirits. Since then, he has collaborated with most of the major Japanese magazines of seinen manga (comics for a mature audience). Among Asano's internationally acclaimed works are: the psychological horror Nijigahara Holograph (2003-2005); the drama Solanin (2005-2006); the existentialistic slice-of-life Goodnight Punpun (2007-2013); the erotic A Girl on the Shore (2009-2013); the sci-fi Dead Dead Demon's Dededede Destruction (2014-2022).

El inicio de Buenas noches, Punpun es, como el resto de la obra, una genialidad. Asano configura a Punpun con todas las aristas que perfilan a un niño, desde el físico hasta la psicología. La idea de diseñar al protagonista con una forma tan simple como la de una especie de pollo antropomorfizado sigue fascinándome a día de hoy: no solo es una hoja en blanco sobre la que resulta sencillo proyectarse como lector; también es una maravillosa alegoría sobre el crecimiento del personaje, que todavía está lejos de alcanzar su madurez. Que el símbolo se refuerce con esa forma que recuerda a las crías de pájaro resulta todavía más astuto.

Mientras tanto, la mente pueril de Punpun se transmite con la misma gracia. No es de extrañar que el tono humorístico permee a cada escena, sea o no absurdo hasta puntos extremos. Desde el filtro subjetivo de un niño soñador que no sabe qué pinta en la vida ni qué puede hacer con ella, Asano se puede permitir insertar una gran dosis de humor que contrastará maravillosamente con la gravedad de las situaciones a medida que avance la trama. No recordaba gran parte de estos primeros capítulos (y para qué negarlo, mi memoria se ha reseteado para el resto también) y me he encontrado a mí mismo riendo a carcajadas con muchas escenas. El humor de Punpun es uno con el que hay que conectar para que funcione efectivamente. y por lo menos conmigo lo ha logrado. Desde el profesor hasta la enciclopedia médica, pasando por las pajas mentales (y físicas) que se monta Punpun, me cuesta recordar escenas donde no me haya partido la caja.

De nuevo, la genialidad de Asano está presente en todos los capítulos. Las fabulosas abstracciones, como esa escena de Tsubasa o Kudasai o la del hospital, siempre me pillan desprevenido, pero el autor logra que, desde el nivel más básico, la obra funcione de lujo. Que la violencia doméstica y la soledad de Punpun se traten desde diseños infantilizados provoca un contraste que muchas veces cuesta digerir, y está claro que Asano sabía a lo que iba. Además de plantar numerosas semillas que se recogerán en posteriores tomos (signo de un autor que tiene planeados varios años de su historia), cada nueva escena con Punpun cumple un propósito narrativo: moldear la persona que se encuentra tras ese pollo deformado y mostrar los pequeños cambios que ya desde pequeños afectan a la psique de cada uno de nosotros. Es cierto que en ocasiones se fuerzan un poco algunas situaciones (lo del vídeo y la fábrica sería una de esas casualidades), pero Asano logra llevarme orgánicamente de un punto a otro y permite que se lo perdone sin problemas.

Vamos, que es un inicio fabuloso. Entre las risas, que no han sido pocas, y la fascinación ante un autor que acierta en todos los palos necesarios, no me equivoco si digo que esa "experiencia Punpun" va a ser tan merecida como la primera vez. Por cierto, qué dibujo, Señor, qué dibujo. Todavía no comprendo el salto de Solanin a Punpun (junto con El fin del mundo y antes del amanecer), pero es algo que existe y me hace muy feliz. Hiperrealista sin saturar la imagen, con un paneling perfecto y un saber hacer en lo visual que poco a poco va mejorando.

Od narednih tankobonova očekujem više, jer je ovo tek uvod u sve što nas očekuje.

Naravno, zbog nekih eksplicitnih scena koje sadrži, kao i dosta odraslih izraza i tema, ne bih preporučio čitanje mladjima. Takodje, scene i atmosfera su ponekad depresivni, ponekad bizarni, smešni, mirni. To varira od scene do scene. Rekao bih da je ova manga namenjena osobama od 16+ godina. Recimo treća ili četvrta godina srednje škole. Opet, sve zavisi od zrelosti čitalaca. Nismo svi podjednako zreli u istom uzrastu. Tako da ostavljam preporuku, ali sa naglaskom na uzrast kome je manga namenjena.

Childhood dystopia: few do it with as much agonizing dedication as Japanese fiction. And 2D culture- by which I include manga/anime/games/light novels and the like- skewing already so much toward a younger cast, a rather unique development that is perhaps paradoxical, raises. Namely, the younger the protagonist, the more likely it is that you are dealing with a truly disturbing work. There are plenty of exceptions, part of this is also selection bias and the fact that some of the most realized examples of this are the creation of Mohiro Kitoh (Shadow Star Narutaru, Bokurano), aka "merchant of misery".

But even discounting that, I submit for your appreciation: Neon Genesis Evangelion (14),Puella Madoka Magica (14), Yuuki Yuna is a Hero (13), Shoujo Kakumei Utena (14) Alien 9 (no age given but 6th grader), Now and Then, Here and There (middle schooler), Tokyo Magnitude 10 (13, with an 8 year old brother), Grave of the Fireflies (14, little sister being 4), LITCHI HIKARI CLUB (14), with the prequel ぼくらの☆ひかりクラブ 上 (in which the boys are in elementary school), インノサン少年十字軍　上巻 Innosan shōnen jūjigun 1 (same mangaka, about the real historical disaster of a children's crusade), Loveless, Volume 01 (12), The Drifting Classroom, Vol. 1 (6th grader), Deadman Wonderland (14).

This is merely a sample but if you ask those who are very much in touch with 2D, to name some truly disturbing titles, it is a safe assumption that quite a few will be those mentioned above.

If you narrow down to genres already prone to such deconstruction, such as war narratives- that are incredibly common- you run into Gundam, the highly prolific franchise that has been around since the late 70's and is already a sub-genre onto itself: even here, one of the darkest entries is helmeted by none other than a 13 year old boy, Uso in Gundam Victory, whose kill count is also one of the highest.

In fact, even if you expand the criteria to include titles that are very much slice-of-life without going into some of the trappings of an incoming apocalypse, evil aliens, war, supernatural horror of the psychological kind, you still have the likes of ハイ☆スピード！ High Speed! (6th, then 7th graders) that while not even remotely as devastating, are still packed with a level of emotional strife that it gives one pause.

Punpun is (probably) best understood in this context. Like many of the above titles (Loveless immediately comes to mind) it is not just a young person but about childhood, its limits, possibilities, anxieties and peculiarities. Not that Punpun is in any way derivative. If there is one thing that this manga is, it is original. You will probably never quite read something like it. Its staying power is great, too, partially because of the artwork.

For starters, there is Punpun, the lead character. Immediately the reader is confronted with highly detailed and incredibly expressive characters. The children in this manga are not the usual super kawaii, borderline chibi cast one inevitably associates with 2D kids. They emote, when they do, with their entire face and body as opposed to the much common template of staking everything in the eyes. They are also not sanitized: they have runny noses, odds are plenty of them would rather not wash if they could, there is a sense of dirty reality to them. Which does not preclude them- in particular Aiko- from shining beautifully when she does smile. But when she does, she reveals a missing tooth.

How about Punpun? Well, he's...a very simplified birdlike take on a stick figure. That is the best way I can describe him. He interacts with everyone else as if he were entirely normal, so a sense of surrealism is established even before the weirdness kicks in. Why is Punpun represented in such an odd way? I have only read the first chapter- up to chapter 12- but it seems it is a matter of self-representation more than anything else. No one reacts to Punpun and his family all looking like this because they do not see him in this light. We, the reader, do, because we are seeing things from Punpun's perspective.

This may also account for how gross and borderline deformed adults are depicted as. Younger adults, such as the very off kilter teacher, still look like normal people. But middle age onward is a hellscape of uncanny valley proportions. Not all children are beautiful, which is already quite rare given that the trend toward Everyone Is Cute is intensified in younger characters, but they are very much human.

All this is set against a hyper realistic background. You can count the tiles on the buildings and if you pay enough attention, you see the kids passing by lettered flags that relate to the plot yet are not even mentioned as such. The town is rendered with such attention for detail that the reader is pulled into this world, almost effortlessly, to sink or swim in it.

I say sink or swim because Punpun is engaged in a kind of mythological recasting of the lead's experiences through intervals of pure, unadulterated weirdness of the cosmic kind. In visual terms, this means that while at heart it is a slice-of-life narrative (at least up to this point), there are mind-bending panels that push the medium of manga for all its worth. This alone would make it something else entirely, but there is much more going on here.

What is Oyasumi Punpun about? That is a very good question. A 6th grader with social anxiety and in many ways extremely naive for his age, falls in love with a girl who just transferred to his school and sets out, through a series of awkward attempts, to make her fall in love with him, all while navigating budding sexuality and family life.

Put this way, it could be many much more conventional titles. But apart from the way in which it is told- Punpun's bizarre borderline hallucination of penis people, for example- the content itself makes it kin to the titles mentioned above. Trigger warnings follow, for abuse, keep that mind:


It is not just Punpun either. Pure childhood dystopia- as in, that does not just flirt with childhood fears but actually makes them a main theme- often covers most of the cast. In this case (more trigger warnings follow):

All this intersects a very Japanese experience, so to speak. Selling overpriced water as part of a trippy cult may seem part of Punpun's peculiarities, until you realize that Kangen Water is precisely this. Whether the reference is meant or is a mere coincidence, it is pointing in the direction of something very real. The highly negative way in which this is viewed may also come across as shocking but criticizing MLMs has been a feature of Japanese culture- and in particular this kind of darker, more visceral kind- for a while. Turn to Welcome to the NHK to see an example of this, or to one of Mori Calliope's song that overtly states, "Internet, business women drawin' up plans/ Why ya try'na pin me?/ For a pyramid scheme?/ I refuse to be a part of any derelict dreams".

As for the cult aspect, it is worth taking into consideration that the manga, while starting in 2007, refers to an older period. We can see this from the lack of internet, VHS tapes and the like. Meaning, it very likely takes place around the time- maybe even immediately after- the Aum Shinrikyo terrorist attacks of 1995. This is not mentioned and it would not surprise me if it is not, but it is floating around, in the zeitgeist.

This ability to allude, refer to, overtly or otherwise, the culture of the day is also very patent in the speech. A lot of times, 2D's speech is somewhat clunky. Ironically, this can actually make it easier to follow and a better entry point for novice students of the language than movies, where characters tend to speak in a much more true to life manner, including allusions galore, many shortenings and highly informal modes of speech (with that said, 2D can be deceptive and even go against the norm, I've known people who had to be weaned out of using "ore", which they picked up through anime, and pretty rude means of expression that are common in certain genres- and are not even seen as rude there, but would not fly in a lot of real life situations- and levels of speech can be all over the place, with high schoolers speaking like pre-Meiji nobles, or being hyper formal; not to mention the often annoying trend of adding quirky nonsense to the end of sentences, which is even routinely spoofed these days).

The kids in Punpun talk very much as kids would. They drag out certain words, shorten others, and live in a world of shared references that can be missed on the reader- if the reader is me, reading the manga in Japanese, as my third language, all the more so- even down to tiny asides. For example, at one point Harumi is encouraging Punpun to climb over a fence by going "Go Tigers" (as in, kana for "Tigers"). I almost certain it is a sports team of some sort, probably baseball as that is what is referred the most, but have no idea which team it actually is.

If the whiplash is great, between so much that is entirely grounded in reality, and the sheer unadulterated weirdness, that is part and parcel of what makes Punpun unique. As opposed to the likes of Cat Soup, where everything is entirely insane and there is little in terms of an interpretative key to be found, Punpun is, in a very real sense, a kind of fever dream in which a lot of issues, individual and collective, are transformed into meta-narratives and interlocking images. These can be read in a self-referential approach- and probably should, if one is to immerse themself fully in the fictional universal- but there is also a wider reading, in which lived experience and the historical moment are tethering points.

Punpun is very self-aware. Is Punpun's looking like a stick figure any weirder than Loveless, Volume 01 signaling virginity through cat ears? Maybe, then again, maybe not.
