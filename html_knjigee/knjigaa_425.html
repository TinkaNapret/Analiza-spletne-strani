Emi has spent her entire life hiding from the creatures that hunt her. The savage earth spirits are determined to kill her before she can become the living host of a goddess, so she stays hidden—until the day she saves the life of one of her hunters.

Shiro isn't the harmless fox spirit she thought he was. He's mysterious, cunning, unpredictable...and now hers to command. He's sworn to pay his debt to her, but he doesn't know who she is. If he finds out, he'll kill her.

But she can't send him away—not yet. Her future isn't what she thought. The lies surrounding her fate have begun to unravel, and she needs answers before time runs out—answers that lie in the spirit realm. Shiro can take her there...if she dares to trust him.

And only then will she find out how deep the gods' treachery runs.

Annette Marie is the author of YA urban fantasy series Steel & Stone, its prequel trilogy Spell Weaver, romantic fantasy Red Winter, and sassy UF series The Guild Codex.

Her first love is fantasy, while fast-paced adventures and tantalizing forbidden romances are her guilty pleasures. She proudly admits she has a thing for dragons, and her editor has inquired as to whether she intends to include them in every single book.

Annette lives in the frozen winter wasteland of Alberta, Canada (okay, it’s not quite that bad). She shares her life with her husband and their furry minion of darkness—sorry, cat—Caesar. When not writing, she can be found elbow-deep in one art project or another while blissfully ignoring all adult responsibilities.

Emi is the Goddess Amaterasu’s kamigakari. It means she is destined to become her human vessel once Amaterasu is ready for her descension, in only a few months. To ensure a smooth transition for her Goddess, Emi spent the past 10 years getting her mind, body, and soul ready to welcome Amaterasu and her immense power. Emi has lived by a strict regime of meditation and prayers, remaining pure of thought and body, not being allowed to eat unhealthy food, do anything that could be dangerous for her health, and not being allowed to be touched by any man. While this sounds like a sad life, Emi is happy with it and proud of the role she will play. That is, until the day she realizes that she does not know everything about Amaterasu’s descension and that Emi will not share her body with her Goddess, she will be erased to make room for her Goddess.

On her quest for answers and to discover and experience everything she missed out on for the past decade, Emi meets Shiro. A Kitsune who should be her enemy since he is a Yokai, and Emi’s Goddess is a Kami, and Yokai are Kamis’ enemies. But Shiro is more than he appears. After he saves Emi’s life and she saves him in return, they become entangled and the more time Emi spends with him, the more she realizes that not all Yokai are evil and that not all Kami are as pure and kind as she believed.

Here are a few elements of the world-building that can be confusing at first, especially if, like me, you are not familiar with the terms:
- There are 4 Amatsukamis: Izanagi, Izanami, Tsukiyomi, and Amaterasu (Emi’s Goddess)
- There are 4 Kunitsukamis: Suzano, Inari, Uzume and Sarutahiko
- The Amatsukamis and Kunitsukamis are like the yin and the yang, they are enemies, but they are both needed on earth to keep the balance.
- Yokais answer to the Kunitsukamis and are enemies of the Amatsukamis and their servants.

The beginning is a little slow, but keep on reading because it is really worth it! There is a glossary at the end, but mostly, the new words and concepts are introduced simply and clearly, and they mostly have to do with traditional clothing or the Gods and Goddesses. The mythology and Asian culture are present everywhere in the book. The world-building is great, without being overly complicated, and the descriptions are vivid and rich.

This story is set in a modern urban fantasy setting, and I kept forgetting about it because it just felt more like a historical fantasy (or straight fantasy) because of all the rituals and traditional clothing and weapons, so I was surprised every time they mentioned cellphones or buses. It is not a bad thing though, it just shows how the author managed to create an immersive world-building, and how removed from everything Emi is.
